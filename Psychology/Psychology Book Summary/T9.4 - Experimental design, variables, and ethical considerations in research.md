#### **21. Experimental Research Design**

One of the most important aspects of psychological research is understanding the different types of research designs. Experimental designs are particularly useful for establishing **cause-and-effect** relationships.

#trytorecall 

1. **Independent Variable (IV)**:
    
    - This is the variable that the experimenter manipulates.
    - It is the presumed cause in the cause-effect relationship.
    - **Example**: In a study examining the effect of sleep on memory, the amount of sleep participants get would be the independent variable.
2. **Dependent Variable (DV)**:
    
    - This is the variable that is measured to assess the effect of the independent variable.
    - It is the presumed effect in the cause-effect relationship.
    - **Example**: In the sleep study, the participants' memory performance (as measured by a test) would be the dependent variable.
3. **Control Variables**:
    
    - These are variables that researchers attempt to keep constant to prevent them from influencing the results.
    - **Example**: In a memory experiment, the testing environment should be kept consistent for all participants.
4. **Random Assignment**:
    
    - This is the process of randomly assigning participants to different experimental conditions (e.g., sleep vs. no sleep).
    - Random assignment helps ensure that the groups are equivalent at the start of the experiment, reducing bias.
5. **Experimental Group**:
    
    - The group of participants who receive the treatment or manipulation (e.g., the group that gets sleep).
6. **Control Group**:
    
    - The group that does not receive the treatment or receives a placebo. It serves as a baseline to compare with the experimental group.

#### **22. Types of Experimental Designs**

#trytorecall 

1. **Between-Subjects Design**:
    
    - Participants are divided into different groups, and each group experiences different conditions (e.g., one group gets sleep, the other does not).
    - **Advantage**: No risk of participants being influenced by previous conditions.
    - **Disadvantage**: More participants are needed to detect differences between groups.
2. **Within-Subjects Design**:
    
    - The same participants experience all conditions of the experiment (e.g., all participants experience both sleep and no-sleep conditions at different times).
    - **Advantage**: Requires fewer participants since each participant acts as their own control.
    - **Disadvantage**: Potential for **carryover effects**, where experiencing one condition influences performance in another.
3. **Quasi-Experimental Design**:
    
    - Used when random assignment is not possible. This type of design still involves comparing groups, but participants are not randomly assigned.
    - **Example**: Comparing test scores between students from different schools where random assignment to schools is impossible.

#### **23. Correlational Research**

In addition to experiments, researchers also use **correlational research** to identify relationships between variables, though correlation cannot determine causation.

- **Positive Correlation**: When one variable increases, the other variable increases as well.
    
    - **Example**: As study time increases, test scores may increase.
- **Negative Correlation**: When one variable increases, the other decreases.
    
    - **Example**: As stress levels increase, quality of sleep may decrease.
- **Zero Correlation**: No relationship exists between the two variables.
    

**Important Note**: A correlation between two variables does not mean one causes the other. There may be a third variable involved, or the relationship might be coincidental.

#### **24. Ethical Considerations in Psychological Research**

Ethical guidelines are crucial in psychological research to protect the well-being, rights, and dignity of participants. These ethical principles are generally overseen by **Institutional Review Boards (IRBs)**.

1. **Informed Consent**:
    
    - Researchers must inform participants about the nature of the study, including potential risks, and obtain their consent to participate.
    - Consent must be given freely and without coercion.
2. **Confidentiality**:
    
    - Researchers must ensure that the personal information and responses of participants are kept confidential.
    - Only aggregated data should be shared publicly.
3. **Deception**:
    
    - In some cases, deception may be necessary for the integrity of the experiment (e.g., in social psychology experiments where full disclosure would alter participant behavior).
    - When deception is used, researchers must **debrief** participants after the study, explaining the true purpose and allowing participants to ask questions.
4. **Debriefing**:
    
    - After the study, participants are informed of the true purpose of the experiment, and any misconceptions due to deception are corrected.
    - Debriefing ensures that participants leave the study without any negative feelings or misconceptions.
5. **Minimizing Harm**:
    
    - Researchers must avoid causing physical or psychological harm to participants. If there are any risks, they must be minimized and justified by the potential benefits of the research.

#### **25. Examples of Ethical Dilemmas in Research**

1. **The Stanford Prison Experiment**:
    
    - This famous study by Philip Zimbardo involved college students who were assigned to play the roles of prisoners and guards in a mock prison setting.
    - The experiment had to be stopped early due to the extreme psychological stress experienced by the participants.
    - **Ethical Issue**: The participants were subjected to emotional distress, raising concerns about the potential harm caused by the study.
2. **Milgram's Obedience Experiment**:
    
    - Stanley Milgram's study on obedience involved participants administering what they believed were painful electric shocks to another person.
    - Many participants experienced significant emotional distress, even though the shocks were not real.
    - **Ethical Issue**: The deception used and the distress experienced by participants led to debates about the study’s ethical implications.
