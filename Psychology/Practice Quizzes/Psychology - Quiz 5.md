## Practice Exam: Introduction to Psychology and its Methods (Hard Level)

### Topic 1: Genes and Evolution

1. Which factor is the **most direct determinant** controlling whether a gene is expressed (turned 'on' or 'off') at a specific moment in time? A. The overall environment experienced by the organism. B. The biochemical environment within a cell. C. The inherited genotype of the organism. D. The specific timing in the organism's development.
    
2. For a property to be recessively inherited, it requires that: A. It is determined by sex-linked chromosomes. B. The property must be passed down by both parents for it to be expressed in the phenotype. C. Only parents who exhibit the feature themselves can pass it to their offspring. D. The individual must have two recessive alleles (homozygous recessive), which is only possible if the trait is not detrimental.
    
3. The **phenotype** of an organism is ultimately determined by which two interacting factors? A. The number of chromosomes inherited and the number of neurons firing. B. The allele pair (dominant/recessive) and the genome. C. The interaction between the organism's **genotype** and the surrounding **environment** (past and present). D. Only the genes (nature), as psychological traits are largely fixed.
    
4. In Darwin’s theory of evolution by natural selection, **variability** among individuals within a species is considered a(n): A. unwanted by-product of sexual reproduction. B. essential condition for adaptive evolutionary change. C. barrier to traits being passed from parents to offspring. D. fixed characteristic that cannot be altered by genetic drift.
    

### Topic 2: The Brain and Nervous System

5. When Xena the Warrior Princess wakes abruptly from a nap to attack an aggressor, her nervous system shifts from the **Parasympathetic nervous system** to the **Sympathetic nervous system**. This scenario illustrates the difference between which two main nervous system states? A. Parasympathetic nervous system during the nap; Sympathetic nervous system during the attack. B. Somatic nervous system during the nap; Autonomic nervous system during the attack. C. Sympathetic nervous system during the nap; Parasympathetic nervous system during the attack. D. Central nervous system (CNS) during the nap; Peripheral nervous system (PNS) during the attack.
    
6. Neurotransmitters from a presynaptic neuron cause an **excitatory effect** on the postsynaptic neuron by: A. increasing the potential difference (hyperpolarization) between the inside and outside of the postsynaptic neuron B. B. increasing the potential difference between the inside and outside of the presynaptic neuron A. C. lowering the potential difference (depolarization) between the inside and outside of the postsynaptic neuron B. D. enhancing the reuptake of the neurotransmitter, thus stopping the signal.
    
7. Damage resulting in **Spatial Neglect**—the inability to attend to stimuli on one side of the environment, often the left—is typically associated with damage to which brain region? A. Left Temporal Lobe (Wernicke's area). B. Right Frontal Lobe (Broca's area). C. Left Parietal Lobe. D. Right Parietal Lobe.
    
8. **Agonist** drugs achieve their effect by performing actions such as **mimicking a neurotransmitter** or: A. increasing the effectiveness of cleanup enzymes. B. decreasing the production of a precursor molecule. C. blocking the receptor sites on the postsynaptic neuron. D. blocking the reuptake process, thereby increasing the amount of neurotransmitter in the synapse.
    
9. Which subcortical structure primarily acts as the **relay station** for almost all sensory information (excluding smell), directing signals to the appropriate regions of the cerebral cortex? A. Amygdala. B. Hippocampus. C. Hypothalamus. D. Thalamus.
    
10. Which statement correctly distinguishes between **Sensory (Afferent)** and **Motor (Efferent)** neurons? A. Afferent neurons are part of the CNS, while Efferent neurons are part of the PNS. B. Afferent neurons detect information from the physical world and send it _to_ the brain, while Efferent neurons transmit signals _away_ from the CNS to control movement. C. Efferent neurons generate action potentials, while Afferent neurons only transmit chemical signals. D. Efferent neurons are part of the somatic nervous system, while Afferent neurons are part of the autonomic nervous system.
    

### Topic 3: Consciousness and Attention

B. brain activity linked to motor preparation starts approximately 0.5 seconds before conscious awareness of the decision to act. C. consciousness requires information to be integrated into the Global Workspace. D. during REM sleep, brain waves resemble waking patterns (Beta waves).

Words heard in the ear that is receiving the unattended stream. C. Words heard in the ear they are instructed to attend to. D. Their internal monologue or conscious thoughts.
    
13. According to the sleep stage descriptions, which stage is characterized by **minimal awareness** and is crucial for physical restoration? A. N1 (light sleep). B. N2 (deeper sleep). C. N3 (deep, slow-wave sleep). D. REM (Rapid Eye Movement).
    
14. The finding that **Franck Tong and colleagues** superimposed images (a house and a face) and brain activity changed depending on whether the participants _reported_ seeing a house or a face, supports the idea that: A. subjective conscious perception is tightly linked to specific patterns of brain activity. B. the brain shuts down complex cognitive processing when presented with conflicting visual input. C. unconscious brain processing (subliminal perception) takes priority over conscious visual input. D. the Global Workspace model prevents any single visual perception from dominating consciousness.

This experience primarily supports the idea that: A. awareness is necessary for planning complex tasks. B. automatic processes do not require sustained conscious awareness. C. the brain shuts down during repetitive tasks (NREM sleep). D. conscious experience is a unitary phenomenon that cannot be divided.

The instantaneous, unintentional reading of the word "RED" when the ink color is blue (Stroop interference). C. A student diligently applying a newly learned, complex mathematical formula to a novel problem. D. Automatically recalling where you parked your car this morning (spatial location).
    

### Topic 4: Sensation and Perception

17. An observer sees two trees of equal size. If the first tree is 20 meters away and the second is 100 meters away, the retinal image of the first tree will be: A. larger than the retinal image of the second tree. B. smaller than the retinal image of the second tree. C. the inverse of the retinal image of the second tree. D. equal to the retinal image of the second tree.
    
18. Which statement regarding the density and location of photoreceptors in the retina is **correct**? A. The density of cones and rods increases from the fovea to the periphery. B. In the fovea, there are mainly rods, while cones are primarily in the periphery. C. There are no cones and rods where the optic nerve exits the eye (the blind spot). D. The fovea contains only rods, optimizing vision in dim light.
    
19. If the distance between a person’s eyes could be artificially increased, this would directly lead to: A. a decrease in binocular disparity. B. an increase in binocular disparity, enhancing depth perception. C. an increase in the effect of motion parallax. D. a reduction in the effect of size constancy.
    
20. When visual information travels from the primary visual cortex to the **parietal lobe**, this pathway is primarily identified as the **"Where system"** or dorsal stream, and its core function relates to: A. object recognition and identification. B. processing spatial location and guiding motor action (the 'How' system). C. identifying the emotional significance of the object. D. processing timing and motion (When system).
    
21. In the auditory process, the transformation of pressure waves (vibrations) into electrical signals (transduction) occurs when the fluid movement in the cochlea causes which structures to bend? A. The eardrum and the oval window. B. The ossicles (hammer, anvil, stirrup). C. The hair cells along the basilar membrane. D. The sensory receptors in the semicircular canals.
    
22. A subject perceives the sequence [ - * - * - * ] as three distinct dash-asterisk pairs. This grouping is primarily governed by the Gestalt principle of: A. Similarity. B. Closure. C. Proximity. D. Continuity.
    

### Topic 5: Learning

23. Which schedule of reinforcement results in behavior being **most resistant to extinction** when reinforcement is eventually stopped? A. Continuous Reinforcement (CR). B. Fixed Ratio (FR). C. Fixed Interval (FI). D. Variable Ratio (VR).
    
24. The **Rescorla-Wagner Model** suggests that associative learning is driven by a **Prediction Error**, which occurs when the animal: A. is exposed to two stimuli simultaneously (Contiguity). B. experiences a discrepancy between the expected Unconditioned Stimulus (US) and the actual US. C. performs a behavior that is consistently followed by a reward (Instrumental Conditioning). D. learns a conditioned response that requires no awareness (Implicit learning).
    
25. The teacher rewards a child by taking away their chore duties (an unpleasant stimulus) every time the child earns an A on a test. This is an example of: A. Positive Reinforcement (adding a reward). B. Positive Punishment (adding an unpleasant stimulus). C. Negative Punishment (removing a desirable stimulus). D. **Negative Reinforcement** (removing an unpleasant stimulus to increase behavior).
    
26. The key conclusion from **Latent Learning** experiments (like Tolman's rats in a maze) is that: A. all learning requires immediate reinforcement for the behavior to be performed. B. learning involves the creation of a cognitive map (mental representation) that may not be demonstrated until motivation is introduced. C. behavior is strengthened only when it is followed by a satisfying outcome (Law of Effect). D. learning is primarily the association between a conditioned stimulus and an unconditioned stimulus.
    
27. The decrease in response exhibited by a child who loses interest in a continuous, familiar stimulus represents which form of non-associative learning? A. Stimulus Generalization. B. Sensory Adaptation. C. **Habituation**. D. Sensitization.
    

### Topic 6: Memory

28. In a memory task, subjects are asked to count backward during the interval between stimulus presentation and recall. The primary purpose of this back-counting task is to: A. increase concentration on the stimulus. B. prevent interference from previously learned information (Proactive Interference). C. keep the subject alert and highly engaged. D. **overwrite or displace the contents of their working (short-term) memory**.
    
29. If a verbal learning study found that **retroactive interference** was a major cause of forgetting, this means the study showed that the probability of remembering information decreased with: A. the amount of information learned previously. B. the strength of the retrieval cues provided. C. the amount of information learned later. D. the increasing delay between encoding and retrieval.
    
30. Which finding provides an indication that **analogical (mental) images** are functionally **not exactly the same as perceptual images**? A. Mental images cannot be easily reinterpreted or restructured into different forms. B. Mental images can be rotated and scanned, similar to physical objects. C. Mental images are functionally identical to symbolic representations (words/numbers). D. Mental images are based solely on shallow visual encoding.
    
31. A student finds that they can better recall facts studied while listening to classical music if they listen to the same music during the exam. This enhanced retrieval is an example of: A. Context-Dependent Memory (environmental cues). B. Semantic Encoding (deep processing). C. The Recency Effect (STM retrieval). D. **State-Dependent Memory** (internal/emotional state cues).
    
32. The name of your primary school teacher is stored in your **Long-Term Memory**. When you consciously attempt to retrieve this name, you are engaged in an **Explicit** memory task. If you were unable to recall _where_ you learned that person's name (e.g., from an old yearbook vs. a conversation), this would be an example of: A. Proactive Interference. B. Memory Bias. C. Blocking. D. **Source Misattribution (Source Amnesia)**.

The desired conclusion (C) to minimize confirmation bias.

A. Method of Tenacity. B. Method of Authority. C. **A Priori Method**. D. Scientific Method.

A. External validity. B. Internal validity. C. **Construct validity**. D. Statistical validity.

C. **Confounding variable** (a systematic threat to internal validity). D. Predictor variable.
