## Practice Exam: Introduction to Psychology and its Methods (Hard Level)

### Topic 1: Genes and Evolution

1. Which mechanism is the **most proximal** influence controlling gene expression within a single cell? A. The inherited genotype. B. The overall environment experienced by the organism. C. The timing in development. D. The biochemical environment inside the cell.
    
2. If the heritability ratio (H) for a specific trait in a population is calculated as 0.7, this means that: A. 70% of the difference in the trait between any two individuals is due to their genetic differences. B. 70% of the observed variation in the trait across the population is attributable to genetic factors. C. Environmental factors play an insignificant role in determining the trait. D. The specific trait is fixed and cannot be altered by changing the environment.
    
3. When a trait is characterized by **polygenic inheritance**, this means that: A. The trait is determined by a single gene pair with highly variable alleles. B. The trait is determined by the interaction of multiple gene pairs and the environment. C. The trait is fixed, such as having two eyes, and is not influenced by environmental factors. D. The expression of the genes is turned 'on' or 'off' primarily by the overall environment.
    
4. The concept of **Reaction Range** in intelligence suggests that: A. Genetic factors solely determine an individual's IQ score, regardless of environment. B. Environmental factors establish the upper and lower limits for intelligence. C. Genes set the boundaries for an individual’s intellectual potential, while environment dictates where they fall within those limits. D. The heritability of intelligence is 1.0, meaning the trait is determined exclusively by nature.
    
5. In Darwin's theory of evolution by natural selection, **variability** among individuals is crucial because: A. It ensures that populations lose old characteristics quickly. B. It acts as an essential condition for certain traits to confer a higher survival and reproductive rate. C. It reduces the chance of spontaneous mutation within the genome. D. It guarantees that all modern organisms share a small set of ancestors.
    

### Topic 2: The Brain and Nervous System

6. What primary distinction allows the **Electroencephalography (EEG)** technique to have high temporal sensitivity but low location sensitivity? A. EEG measures magnetic fields, unlike fMRI which measures blood oxygenation. B. EEG records the amplified electrical waves generated by large groups of neurons across the scalp, making precise source localization difficult. C. EEG requires the injection of a radioactive tracer, limiting its frequency of use. D. EEG measures brain activity in response to a specific 'event' (ERP), whereas other techniques measure baseline activity.
    
7. An **inhibitory signal** from a presynaptic neuron decreases the likelihood of a postsynaptic neuron firing by: A. increasing the activity of cleanup enzymes in the synapse. B. increasing the potential difference (hyperpolarization) between the inside and outside of the postsynaptic neuron. C. causing the postsynaptic neuron to depolarize rapidly. D. binding to autoreceptors, thereby ceasing the release of the neurotransmitter.
    
8. The difference between **motor neurons (efferent)** and **sensory neurons (afferent)** is that: A. Sensory neurons carry impulses within the CNS, while motor neurons carry impulses to the body. B. Sensory neurons detect information from the world and send it _to_ the brain, while motor neurons direct muscles to act (control movement). C. Motor neurons generate both electrical and chemical signals, while sensory neurons generate only electrical signals. D. Sensory neurons are part of the autonomic nervous system, while motor neurons are part of the somatic nervous system.
    
9. Which structure is critically involved in **emotional processing**, particularly **fear** and aggression, and plays a role in forming associated memories? A. Thalamus (relay station). B. Hypothalamus (homeostasis). C. Hippocampus (new explicit memories). D. **Amygdala** (emotional processing).
    
10. Damage to the **left temporal lobe**, specifically **Wernicke's area**, would most likely result in a language disorder characterized by: A. Difficulty producing speech, though comprehension remains intact (Expressive Aphasia/Broca's). B. Difficulty understanding language, resulting in fluent but often meaningless speech (Receptive Aphasia). C. Complete loss of motor control over complex physical behaviors. D. Impaired spatial abilities and difficulty perceiving facial emotions (Right Hemisphere).
    

### Topic 3: Consciousness

Conscious experience arises from integrating information into the Global Workspace. D. The brain shuts down complex thinking during deep sleep stages (N3).

The unresponsive state is physiologically induced, whereas the minimally conscious state is psychologically induced. B. The unresponsive state involves wakefulness but no conscious awareness, while the minimally conscious state may show limited, fluctuating awareness. C. Both states involve full awareness but an inability to move or communicate (Locked-in syndrome). D. The unresponsive state is irreversible, while the minimally conscious state guarantees a full recovery.

Only controlled, goal-driven (endogenous) attention can fully suppress irrelevant information. C. Important unattended information, such as one's own name, can break through the filter of selective attention. D. Automatic processes (like identifying words) require complete conscious awareness.

The immediate reading of the word 'BLUE' printed in red ink (Stroop effect interference). C. A musician sight-reading a highly complex, new piece of music. D. Automatically recalling the route to a familiar school.

### Topic 4: Sensation and Perception

Detect spatial orientation and balance. C. Transmit vibrations from the eardrum to the oval window. D. Determine the frequency and pitch of the sound wave.

Similarity (grouping based on shared features). B. Continuity (perceiving smooth, continuous patterns). C. **Proximity** (grouping objects near each other). D. Closure (mentally filling in gaps to see a complete object).

Processing spatial location and guiding motor interaction (the 'How' system). C. Language comprehension. D. Identifying the emotional significance of the object (the 'Why' system).
    
19. Which statement accurately describes the relationship between the **Trichromatic Theory** and the **Opponent-Process Theory** of color vision? A. They are competing theories that explain the same stage of visual processing. B. Trichromatic theory explains color perception based on three cone types, while Opponent-Process theory explains color afterimages by processing opposing color pairs (e.g., blue-yellow) at later stages. C. Both theories exclusively explain color processing in the retina. D. Opponent-Process theory is exclusively concerned with how pitch is encoded in the auditory cortex.

It acts as an automatic, bottom-up processing mechanism based on retinal input. C. It involves a mental predisposition influenced by expectations, context, or prior knowledge that biases top-down interpretation. D. It causes sensory adaptation to constant stimuli over time.
    

### Topic 5: Learning

21. The **Rescorla-Wagner Model** suggests that conditioning only occurs if: A. The conditioned stimulus (CS) and the unconditioned stimulus (US) are presented simultaneously (Contiguity). B. The subject experiences a positive or negative **Prediction Error** based on whether the US is expected or unexpected following the CS. C. The behavior is strengthened by a reward (Law of Effect). D. The learning involves the unconscious acquisition of a cognitive map (Latent Learning).
    
22. In operant conditioning, which schedule of reinforcement leads to the **fastest extinction** when reinforcement is stopped? A. Fixed Ratio (FR). B. Variable Ratio (VR). C. Continuous Reinforcement (CR). D. Variable Interval (VI).
    
23. If a child witnesses another child being punished for hitting a toy, and subsequently avoids hitting the toy themselves, this is an example of: A. Modeling. B. Latent Learning. C. Negative Punishment. D. **Vicarious Learning**.
    
24. What psychological phenomenon explains why a dog trained to salivate at the sound of a bell might also salivate slightly at the sound of a similar-pitched chime? A. Extinction. B. Discrimination. C. **Stimulus Generalization**. D. Spontaneous Recovery.
    
25. A researcher wants to establish a complex, novel behavior in a pigeon using operant conditioning. The most effective technique to achieve this gradual behavior change is: A. Continuous Reinforcement. B. **Shaping** (reinforcing successive approximations). C. Extinction. D. Negative Punishment.
    

### Topic 6: Memory

26. The key finding that **Mental Images cannot easily be reinterpreted** supports the idea that: A. Mental images are functionally identical to perceptual images. B. Mental images can be rotated and scanned like physical images. C. **Analogical (mental) images are not exactly the same as perceptual images**. D. Mental images rely on symbolic representation rather than analogical representation.

A. **Maintenance Rehearsal:** Repeatedly reciting the words aloud (Acoustic Encoding). B. **Shallow Processing:** Focusing only on the font and capitalization of the text (Visual Encoding). C. **Deep Processing:** Relating the information to existing personal knowledge and generating examples (Elaborative/Semantic Encoding). D. **Chunking:** Grouping items into units of 7 ± 2 for immediate short-term recall.
    
28. If a student studies in a state of high anxiety and finds they can only recall the information during the exam when they are similarly anxious, this phenomenon is best explained by: A. Context-Dependent Memory (environmental cues). B. Proactive Interference (old information blockage). C. **State-Dependent Memory** (internal/emotional state cues). D. Retrograde Amnesia (pre-injury memory loss).
    
29. The **Recency Effect** observed in a free-recall task (remembering the last items best) is primarily attributed to the contents of which memory system? A. Sensory Memory (Iconic/Echoic). B. Explicit (Declarative) Long-Term Memory. C. **Short-Term/Working Memory**. D. Procedural Implicit Memory.
    
30. The cognitive error where an individual remembers a piece of information but fails to recall where, when, or how it was acquired (e.g., confusing a memory from a news report with a personal event) is known as: A. Memory Bias. B. Misinformation Effect. C. **Source Misattribution** (Source Amnesia). D. Blocking (Tip-of-the-tongue phenomenon).
