### **Topic 1: Genes and Evolution (Questions 1–5)**

1. How does methylation primarily contribute to the variability observed in the phenotype of monozygotic twins, despite them sharing the exact same genotype? A. It alters the underlying DNA sequence to create new alleles. B. It controls the extent to which a specific gene is transcribed into a protein, essentially turning genes on or off. C. It increases the total number of chromosomes within the cell nucleus, affecting gene dose. D. It dictates whether inheritance follows dominant or recessive patterns for that specific trait.
    
2. If a complex psychological trait is influenced by polygenetic inheritance, what does a heritability estimate of 0.30 specifically suggest about that trait in the context of the population studied? A. 70% of the individual's expression of the trait is predetermined by environmental factors. B. The trait is subject to strong environmental fixation and is unlikely to evolve. C. 30% of the variance observed in the population's scores is attributable to genetic differences. D. The trait is determined by three specific gene pairs.
    
3. The statement that evolution promotes the survival of genes rather than the survival of individuals is best understood by recognizing that evolutionary fitness is measured by: A. The organism's capacity to develop complex, non-rigid behavioral patterns. B. The successful replication and propagation of genetic material across generations. C. The ability of the individual organism to achieve homeostasis throughout its lifespan. D. The rate at which the organism reduces the number of pseudogenes within its genome.
    
4. Research showing that rats raised in enriched environments develop thicker cortices supports which central idea regarding the relationship between nature and nurture? A. Genes predispose biology but determine behavior, regardless of experience. B. Early life experiences solely determine neurological structures, overriding genetics. C. There is a continuous, interactive effect between genetic predisposition and environmental experience. D. The primary source of human variability is fixed genotype that resists environmental change.
    
5. Which condition for evolution by natural selection is most directly challenged if all members of a specific population were found to have identical, fixed phenotypes? A. The trait associated with the advantage is heritable. B. There is variation among individuals of a population. C. Individuals with certain traits survive and reproduce at higher rates. D. The genome is shared among ancestors.
    

### **Topic 2: The Brain and the Nervous System (Questions 6–10)**

6. Since a neuron's electrical impulse adheres to the all-or-none law, how does the nervous system primarily encode a stimulus perceived as _more intense_? A. By increasing the electrical charge (amplitude) of a single action potential. B. By increasing the velocity at which the neural impulse travels down the axon. C. By raising the firing rate (frequency) and increasing the number of neurons activated. D. By decreasing the potential difference between the inside and outside of the presynaptic neuron.
    
7. A pharmaceutical drug designed to treat anxiety works by binding to GABA receptors and increasing GABA's inhibitory effect. This drug is acting as a(n): A. Antagonist, by enhancing the re-uptake of the neurotransmitter. B. Agonist, by blocking the receptors. C. Antagonist, by enhancing the cleanup enzymes. D. Agonist, by mimicking or enhancing the neurotransmitter’s action.
    
8. Damage to the Primary Visual Cortex would most severely impair which function, and in which brain lobe is this cortex located? A. Hearing and language comprehension; Temporal Lobe. B. Spatial awareness and touch; Parietal Lobe. C. Vision; Occipital Lobe. D. Speech production and planning; Frontal Lobe.
    
9. A split-brain patient is shown a picture of a hammer presented only to their left visual field. Which action would the patient be able to perform due to the lateralization of function? A. Verbally report seeing the hammer (Left H. needed for speech). B. Use their right hand to select the hammer from a group of objects (Right hand controlled by Left H.). C. Use their left hand to select the hammer from a group of objects (Left hand controlled by Right H.). D. Process the image using both the Left and Right hemispheres simultaneously.
    
10. The Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) regulates involuntary functions. If a person abruptly encounters a stressful situation, leading to increased heart rate and dilation of the pupils, which sub-system of the ANS is primarily responsible for mobilizing this energy? A. The Somatic Nervous System (voluntary control). B. The Afferent (Sensory) nervous system. C. The Parasympathetic Nervous System (calming/rest and digest). D. The Sympathetic Nervous System (arousing/fight or flight).
    

### **Topic 3: Consciousness (Questions 11–14)**

11. The "explanation gap" in consciousness, often referred to as the Hard Problem, focuses on the fundamental difficulty of explaining: A. How the brain can process information without conscious attention (subliminal perception). B. Why unconscious processing initiates motor actions before conscious decision making (Readiness Potential). C. How subjective, qualitative experiences (qualia) arise from physical brain activity. D. The shift from controlled (focused) processing to automatic processing through practice.
    
12. The ability to attend selectively to only one voice among many, yet still have personally relevant information (like hearing your own name) break through from the unattended input, is known as the: A. Change blindness phenomenon. B. Global Workspace Model. C. Cocktail party phenomenon. D. Somatoparaphrenia.
    
13. Which sleep theory posits that sleep is essential because it allows for the strengthening of synaptic connections formed during learning, thereby converting potential memory contents into a more permanent state? A. Circadian Rhythm Theory. B. Restorative Theory. C. Facilitation of Learning Theory. D. The dual process theory (REM/NREM).
    
14. When a person must continuously increase the amount of a drug consumed to achieve the original intended effect, they are experiencing which physiological phenomenon? A. Withdrawal. B. Physical or psychological dependence. C. Addiction. D. Tolerance.
    

### **Topic 4: Sensation and Perception (Questions 15–19)**

15. The process where light energy is absorbed by pigments in the rods and cones and converted into an electrical signal carried by ganglion cells to the brain is known as: A. Sensory adaptation. B. Perceptual organization. C. Transduction. D. The just noticeable difference (JND).
    
16. A patient with visual agnosia can accurately describe the location of a cup and successfully reach for it (relying on the Dorsal stream), but cannot verbally identify it as a "cup" or describe its color/shape. This deficit suggests damage primarily to the: A. Where pathway (Dorsal stream, path to parietal cortex). B. What pathway (Ventral stream, path to temporal cortex). C. Primary somatosensory cortex. D. Optic nerve.
    
17. When a door opens away from you, the image cast on your retina continuously changes shape, yet you perceive the door as retaining its original rectangular form. This stable perception despite continuously changing proximal stimuli is an example of: A. Lightness constancy. B. Size constancy. C. Shape constancy. D. Binocular disparity.
    
18. Which of the following monocular depth cues relies on the principle that if one object partially blocks the view of another object, the obscured object is perceived as being farther away? A. Linear perspective. B. Motion parallax. C. Occlusion. D. Convergence.
    
19. In a word recognition task, participants are briefly shown "__eel is on the orange." They reliably report seeing the word "Peel." This use of prior context and expectation to construct meaning from ambiguous sensory input illustrates the critical role of: A. Bottom-up processing (starting with sensory receptors). B. Sensory adaptation (tendency to respond less to unchanging stimuli). C. Top-down processing. D. Dichotic listening.
    

### **Topic 5: Learning (Questions 20–24)**

20. According to the cognitive view of classical conditioning, formalized by the Rescorla-Wagner model, learning occurs best when the Conditioned Stimulus (CS) is highly effective as a: A. Stimulus that is contiguous with the Unconditioned Stimulus (US) in time. B. Predictor (contingency) of the occurrence of the US. C. Response that immediately precedes the Reinforcer. D. Stimulus that is similar to previously learned stimuli (generalization).
    
21. A cleaner gets paid only after cleaning an average of 15 rooms, though the exact number of rooms required changes randomly each day. This reinforcement schedule is known to produce the highest rates of persistent responding and is most analogous to gambling. This is a: A. Fixed-Ratio (FR) schedule. B. Fixed-Interval (FI) schedule. C. Variable-Interval (VI) schedule. D. Variable-Ratio (VR) schedule.
    
22. In the context of operant conditioning, reinforcing successive approximations of a desired complex behavior until the target behavior is achieved is referred to as: A. Extinction. B. Modeling. C. Shaping. D. Latent learning.
    
23. A child refuses to eat vegetables (Response). To stop this behavior, the parent takes away the child's dessert (Removal of pleasant stimulus). If the child subsequently refuses vegetables less often, this consequence is an example of: A. Positive Punishment (Adding unpleasant consequence). B. Negative Punishment (Removing pleasant consequence). C. Positive Reinforcement (Adding pleasant consequence). D. Negative Reinforcement (Removing unpleasant consequence).
    
24. The phenomenon of **Learned Helplessness** is associated with a breakdown in learning contingency. This occurs when an organism learns that: A. A reward (reinforcer) is not contingent on the immediate response. B. The response consistently precedes the punishment (positive contingency). C. There is no predictive relationship between their responses and the consequences (uncontrollability). D. Only continuous reinforcement leads to high rates of behavior.
    

### **Topic 6: Memory (Questions 25–29)**

25. A memory technique that allows an individual to significantly expand the limited capacity of short-term memory (the "magic number seven" limit) by grouping discrete units of information into meaningful wholes (e.g., remembering a sequence of numbers as dates) is known as: A. Elaborative rehearsal. B. Chunking (Recoding). C. Maintenance rehearsal. D. Long-term potentiation (LTP).
    
26. Patient H.M., despite suffering from severe anterograde amnesia (inability to form new long-term declarative memories), retained the ability to learn new motor skills and habits. This preserved ability relies on which memory system? A. Episodic memory. B. Semantic memory. C. Procedural memory (Implicit). D. Working memory.
    
27. A person remembers an event vividly but attributes the memory to the wrong time, place, or circumstances (e.g., believing they saw an event happen when they only read about it in the newspaper). This specific form of memory distortion is called: A. Suggestibility. B. Memory bias. C. Source misattribution. D. Absentmindedness.
    
28. A student studies for a Spanish test late at night. The next morning, she finds that the Spanish terms she learned make it harder to retrieve the French vocabulary she had learned the previous month. This interference is an example of: A. Proactive interference (Old hinders new). B. Storage decay. C. Retroactive interference (New hinders old). D. Blocking.
    
29. Flashbulb memories are clear and vivid memories of emotionally significant events. However, research demonstrates that despite their vividness, these memories are prone to: A. Complete repression, making them inaccessible over time. B. Memory bias and distortion over time, similar to regular memories. C. Exclusive storage in the amygdala, bypassing the hippocampus. D. Immediate decay after reconsolidation.

A critic argues that this measure suffers from **mono-operation bias**. This deficiency most directly threatens the study's: A. External validity (Generalization). B. Statistical validity (Effect size). C. Internal validity (Causation). D. Construct validity (Measurement precision).
    
45. In an experiment, the researcher intends to compare two treatments but mistakenly administers one treatment in a clean, well-lit lab and the other in a cluttered, noisy storage room. If the outcomes differ, the systematic variation of the unintended factor (lab environment) with the IV (treatment) creates a threat to internal validity called a: A. Selection effect. B. Design confound. C. Placebo effect. D. Attrition threat.

Used high-quality, reliable operational definitions. C. Successfully ruled out alternative causal explanations. D. Ensured that the results generalize to individuals, settings, or times beyond the study itself.

### **Topic 12: Correlations and Experiments (Questions 51–54)**

This ambiguity is specifically known as the: A. Directionality problem. B. Restriction of range problem. C. Third-variable problem / Spurious correlation. D. Non-linear relationship problem.
    
52. In an experiment, a difficult memory test is given to participants. The test is so challenging that almost every participant scores near zero, regardless of the level of the independent variable manipulation. This failure to detect a true effect due to the DV measure being too insensitive at the low end is known as a: A. Ceiling effect (scores cluster at the top). B. Floor effect (scores cluster at the bottom). C. History threat. D. Design confound.
    
53. Researchers use a longitudinal **cross-lagged-panel procedure** to establish temporal precedence between Variable A (measured at T1, T2) and Variable B (measured at T1, T2). Evidence that Variable A causes Variable B exists if: A. The simultaneous correlation $r(\text{A at } t1, \text{B at } t1)$ is high. B. The correlation $r(\text{A at } t1, \text{B at } t2)$ is significantly greater than the correlation $r(\text{B at } t1, \text{A at } t2)$. C. The correlation $r(\text{B at } t1, \text{A at } t2)$ is significantly greater than $r(\text{A at } t1, \text{B at } t2)$. D. The study uses random assignment to control for selection effects.
    
54. The crucial requirement that differentiates a true experiment from a correlational study is that a true experiment must utilize **manipulation** of the Independent Variable in order to: A. Ensure high external validity through random sampling. B. Allow the researcher to establish temporal precedence (cause precedes effect). C. Mitigate the third-variable problem through statistical control. D. Guarantee the use of ratio or interval measurement scales.
